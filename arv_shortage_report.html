Executive summary

The ARV shortage conversation is highly sensitive because it affects continuity of HIV treatment. The strongest engagement spike came from mass-media amplification of the DOH’s procurement of roughly 1.2 million bottles of ARV/TLD medicines. However, social conversation is more personal and critical: patients describe fear, uncertainty, travel burden, and frustration with two-week or shorter refill limits.

Conversation risk is concentrated around three perceived failures: procurement reliability, treatment hub buffer-stock planning, and clarity of patient guidance. Posts also show a split between reassurance narratives, such as “treatment is available,” and lived-experience narratives, such as patients reporting reduced quantities, inconsistent hub information, or asking whether they can transfer hubs or buy privately.

Recommended posture: move from generic reassurance to operational transparency. The public needs concrete supply timelines, refill rules by location, fallback routes, inter-hub transfer guidance, and a visible escalation channel for patients at risk of interruption.

Key narrative split

Institutional narrative

DOH and advocacy/hub communications emphasize that procurement is underway, delays were linked to supply-chain disruption, treatment remains available, and temporary rationing is meant to stretch stocks.

Community narrative

Patients and advocates focus on daily treatment continuity, fear of missed doses, questions about where to refill, skepticism toward “do not panic” messaging, and whether hub-level planning should have prevented two-week rationing.

Implication: The gap is not only information. It is trust. Communications must prove control of the situation through specific operational steps, not broad assurances.

Risk 1: Trust erosion

Patients perceive a gap between public reassurance and actual refill experiences. “Treatment is available” is being tested against reports of rationing, alternative regimens, and inconsistent hub-level information.

Risk 2: Access inequity

Shorter refill cycles impose disproportionate costs on provincial clients, people with limited mobility, and those who cannot repeatedly travel to treatment hubs.

Risk 3: Political escalation

Calls for accountability, OHAT scrutiny, and legislative inquiry can keep the issue alive even after shipments arrive unless the supply recovery is transparent and measurable.

Suggested holding statement

“We understand the fear caused by any uncertainty around ARV access. ARV treatment must remain continuous, and our priority is to ensure no patient is left without medication. We are publishing daily updates on shipment status, available refill options, affected hubs, and emergency assistance channels. Patients whose supply will run out within the next [X] days should contact [channel] with their hub, regimen, remaining pills, location, and contact number so we can route them to available supply or delivery support.”
